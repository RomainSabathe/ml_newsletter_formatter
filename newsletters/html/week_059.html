General Machine Learning

Top 8 trends from ICLR 2019
Chip Huyen

If the now classic and stunning notes from David Abel are too much to handle, then you will like this summary of ICLR 2019. There is a fair selection of works on unsupervised learning, reinforcement learning and bio-inspired deep learning. The author's good understanding of the field makes it easier to understand where the community is heading towards (model-based reinforcement learning; meta-learning for unsupervised tasks; RNNs being less attractive compared to the Transformer, etc.).

A 2019 guide to 3D Pose Estimation
Sudharshan Chandra Babu

Yet another great post from Sudharshan Chandra Babu who already wrote about 2D pose estimation last month. The format is similar: it is a paper-by-paper survey of some of the latest and most popular works on 3D pose estimation.

[arXiv] Object Detection in 20 Years: A Survey
University of Michigan, Beihang University, Carleton University

[...]. If we think of today's object detection as a technical aesthetics under the power of deep learning, then turning back the clock 20 years we would witness the wisdom of cold weapon era. This paper extensively reviews 400+ papers of object detection in the light of its technical evolution, spanning over a quarter-century's time (from the 1990s to 2019). A number of topics have been covered in this paper, including the milestone detectors in history, detection datasets, metrics, fundamental building blocks of the detection system, speed up techniques, and the recent state of the art detection methods. This paper also reviews some important detection applications, such as pedestrian detection, face detection, text detection, etc, and makes an in-deep analysis of their challenges as well as technical improvements in recent years.

Gans & Adversarial Attacks

[arXiv] Adversarial Examples Are Not Bugs, They Are Features
MIT

[...] We demonstrate that adversarial examples can be directly attributed to the presence of non-robust features: features derived from patterns in the data distribution that are highly predictive, yet brittle and incomprehensible to humans. After capturing these features within a theoretical framework, we establish their widespread existence in standard datasets. Finally, we present a simple setting where we can rigorously tie the phenomena we observe in practice to a misalignment between the (human-specified) notion of robustness and the inherent geometry of the data.

Everything Else

Matrices as Tensor Network Diagrams
Tai-Danae Bradley

This short post is an introduction to a method that represents tensors in any dimension and their associated operations by using graphs. The graphs are then called Tensor Network Diagrams (or string diagrams depending on your field...). I love this kind of simple yet neat idea. The author presents a few potential applications: a visual representation of SVD and a 'proof' that trace is invariant to cyclic permutations. You should check out the rest of the author's website, there are a ton of interesting articles.
