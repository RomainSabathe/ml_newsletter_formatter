General Machine Learning

An Intuitive Guide to Optimal Transport (Part I and II)
Luca Ambrogioni

The whole field of machine learning is exploring more and more the properties of optimal transport as an alternative to Kullback-Leibler divergence. This post is a short but clear and interesting introduction to optimal transport. The author offers different views (from intuition to discrete formulation and continuous formulation). The second part deals more specifically with a highlight of last year: the Wasserstein GAN. On the same subject, you can also find a recent application of optimal transport to NLP in the paper Wasserstein is all you need. To go even further, check out the workshop presented at NIPS 2017, A Primer on Optimal Transport by Cuturi and Solomon.

GINN: Geometric Illustrations for Neural Networks
Luke Darlow

This blog post offers a rather entertaining demo that you can find here. A shallow fully-connected ReLU-activated network is trained to output a fixed predefined shape. The demo lets you see how the decision frontier of the different ReLU units evolve over time. As the post indicates, we can observe how it is the biases which are mainly modified in the first iterations and how deeper layers build a more comprehensive representation of the image.

Neural Processes in PyTorch
Chris Ormandy

A few weeks ago, we shared a blog post giving an introduction to one of DeepMind's latest work: neural processes. The goal of this technique is to model a distribution of functions (rather than a distribution of vectors) and to use neural networks to do so. In other words, it can be seen as the 'deep learning' equivalent to gaussian processes. This blog post offers a more hands-on view as code is made available to create neural processes.

Optimisation & Learning Theory

ICM 2018 - Dynamical, symplectic and stochastic perspectives on optimization
Michael Jordan

Our topic is the relationship between dynamical systems and optimization. [...] We aim to build some new connections in this general area, studying aspects of gradient-based optimization from a continuous-time, variational point of view. We go beyond classical gradient flow to focus on second-order dynamics, aiming to show the relevance of such dynamics to optimization algorithms that not only converge, but converge quickly.

A 1 hour-long presentation given at the International Congress of Mathematians that was held in early August this year.

Hamiltonian Descent Methods
University of Oxford, DeepMind

We propose a family of optimization methods that achieve linear convergence using first-order gradient information and constant step sizes on a class of convex functions much larger than the smooth and strongly convex ones. [...] They are first-order in the sense that they require only gradient computation. [...] In sum, these methods expand the class of convex functions on which linear convergence is possible with first-order computation.

Fast Computation of Uncertainty in Deep Learning
Mohammad Emtiyaz Khan

These are the slides given at a presentation in Singapore. They can serve as an introduction to the paper the author published at ICLM 2018 with his colleagues (see here). They perturb the gradient updates during optimisation in such a way that performing variational inference is made easier.

Reinforcement Learning

Counterfactual Regret Minimization – the core of Poker AI beating professional players
Kamil Czarnogórski

In recent years, artificial intelligence was able to beat professional poker players in Heads Up Texas Hold'em. This post goes over the progress that has been made over the last few years and offers a detailed explanation of counterfactual regret minimization: the core algorithm that won those games. Dedicate a bit of free time as the post is lengthy (in the good sense!).

Simple diagrams of convoluted neural networks
Piotr Migdał

One can be surprised to notice that there is still no common, unified way of graphically representing neural networks in papers. We could argue that the diversity of architectures can't be represented though a unique type of visualisation. While this blog post does not offer an explicit solution, its author does a great job at going over different types of visualisations that have been tested in the past, while highlighting their respective strenghts and weaknesses. I particularly liked the end of the post where you will find a list of tools that can help you build a good visualisation of your architecture.
